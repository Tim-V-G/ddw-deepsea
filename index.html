Graceful sea turtles

feed on seagrasses and help control jellyfish populations.
Conservation efforts aim to protect these endangered species and their habitats in the epipelagic zone.

Small fish like sardines, anchovies, and mackerel are abundant in the epipelagic zone.
They play a crucial role in the marine food chain,
feeding on plankton and providing a food source for larger predators.
Gelatinous jellyfish are common in this zone, known for their stinging cells. Some species form blooms,
impacting the ecosystem by outcompeting other organisms for food resources.
Squid, including the Humboldt squid, are agile predators with bioluminescent patterns.
They use their ability to rapidly change color for hunting and evading predators.
Dolphins and whales forage in this zone, showcasing complex behaviors and communication.
Whales, such as humpbacks, feed on small fish and krill.

The deepest dive by a human!

Ahmed Gabr's extraordinary dive to a depth of 332.35 meters, or 1,090 feet, took place in the waters of the Red Sea, specifically off the coast of Dahab, Egypt. This record-breaking dive occurred on September 18, 2014. Gabr meticulously planned and prepared for this endeavor over a four-year period, focusing on physical fitness, equipment testing, and extensive dive training to ensure his safety and success. The dive was officially recognized by the Guinness World Records, solidifying Gabr's place in history as the deepest scuba diver.
